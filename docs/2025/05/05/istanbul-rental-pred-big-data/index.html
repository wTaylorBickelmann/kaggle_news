Headline: “Spark, Flags and a Dash of Leakage: A Surprising Take on Istanbul Rental Price Modeling”

In a recent Kaggle notebook, a data enthusiast tackles Istanbul’s apartment‐for‐rent dataset with an unexpectedly simple yet controversial strategy. By leaning heavily on PySpark’s ML pipeline, the author glues together a handful of crafted features, wires them into a linear regression, and turns out an almost too-good-to-be-true R² score.

Key Steps and Techniques
1. Spark Setup and EDA
• Ingested 30,000+ listings via SparkSession for scalable exploration.
• Ran basic “describe” statistics and a seaborn correlation heatmap on a Pandas slice to spot strongest relationships among rooms, area, age, floor, and price.

2. Feature Engineering “Brag Book”
• total_rooms = rooms + living rooms
• Boolean floor flags: is_ground_floor (floor == 0), is_top_floor (floor == building’s max), is_underground (floor < 0)
• Risky_floor: marks old (>20 years) flats on high floors (>5)
• m2_per_room = area ÷ total_rooms
• **Price_per_m2** = price ÷ area (the notebook’s most controversial “feature,” since price_per_m2 and price are mathematically intertwined)

3. Vector Assembly and Linear Regression
• Compiled these 12 predictors into a Spark ML VectorAssembler.
• Split 80/20 for train/test, fit a basic LinearRegression, and evaluated via R².

What’s Unusual (and a Little Fishy)
Embedding price_per_m2 as an input effectively leaks information about the target (total price). Because price = (price_per_m2) × (area), the model sees a nearly perfect proxy for what it’s asked to predict. The result: an R² that soars into the 0.90–0.95 range—impressively high for a purely linear approach on heterogeneous real-estate data, but achieved at the expense of real predictive integrity.

Why It Matters
While the notebook showcases the power of Spark’s end-to-end pipeline and smart indicator features (like risky_floor), it also illustrates a classic cautionary tale: introduce a feature that’s too close to your target and you’ll “predict” noise instead of learning patterns. As a demo of feature-engineering finesse, it’s brilliant; as a production recipe, it’s a reminder that data leakage can turn your model into little more than a calculator.
